# Art Bible v1.0

Art Philosophy
The visual identity of Mytherra should evoke:
Wonder
Nostalgia
Mystery
Warmth
Hope
Players should feel invited to explore.
The world should never feel bleak for the sake of being dark.
Beauty and mystery should coexist.
Artistic Inspiration
Primary inspirations:
Classic SNES and PlayStation-era JRPGs
Painterly fantasy illustrations
American national park landscapes
Appalachian folk architecture
Storybook illustrations
Handcrafted tabletop maps
The goal is not realism.
The goal is emotional realism.

Lighting
Lighting tells the story.
Never use flat lighting.
Lanterns produce warm light.
Shrines emit soft sacred light.
Echoes cast unnatural shadows.
Moonlight is cool.
Sunrise uses long golden lighting.
Every important scene should have memorable lighting.
Character Design
Characters should be expressive without becoming exaggerated.
Silhouettes should be recognizable.
Every major NPC should be identifiable from shape alone.
Clothing reflects:
region
occupation
culture
personality
Lantern Keeper Uniform
Every Lantern Keeper carries:
Traveling coat
Utility satchel
Weathered boots
Spirit Lantern
Journal
Simple gloves
Regional insignia
Players personalize these over time.
Creature Design
Monsters should not resemble generic fantasy creatures.
Every creature should communicate:
regional history
emotional corruption
environmental influence
Example:
Echo Raven
Instead of a zombie bird…
Feathers resemble smoke.
Eyes reflect moonlight.
Its shape appears unstable.
The design tells the player it is a wounded memory.
Guardian Design
Guardians should inspire awe rather than fear.
Massive scale.
Graceful movement.
Natural materials.
Minimal ornamentation.
Examples:
Thunderbird
Clouds form around its wings.
The White Buffalo
Prairie flowers bloom where it walks.
Mother Cypress
Trees grow from her shoulders.
Old Stone Bear
Entire hillsides shift as it moves.
Architecture
Buildings should feel handcrafted.
Materials:
Wood
Stone
Iron
Brick
Copper
No shiny fantasy castles.
Communities should appear built by generations of ordinary people.
User Interface
The interface should disappear into the world.
Materials:
Wood
Leather
Brass
Paper
Lantern glass
Avoid futuristic effects.
Menus should resemble pages from a field journal.
Icons
Hand-painted appearance.
Simple silhouettes.
Readable at small sizes.
Natural colors.
Avoid neon.
Animation Philosophy
Movement should communicate weight.
Characters should breathe.
Lanterns should sway.
Trees should move gently.
Water should flow continuously.
Nothing should feel perfectly static.
Small environmental motion makes the world feel alive.

Asset Standards
Player sprite: 32×32 pixels per frame.
NPC sprites: 32×32.
Enemy battle sprites: 128×128.
Boss sprites: 256×256 or larger.
Portraits: 512×512.
Item icons: 64×64.
Environment tiles: 16×16 or 32×32.
All assets should support replacement without changing code.
Visual Storytelling
The environment should answer questions before dialogue does.
Examples:
A broken bridge tells of isolation.
A restored shrine tells of hope.
Fresh flowers at an abandoned memorial hint that someone still visits.
Players should infer stories from what they see.
Signature Visual Motifs
Every player should associate Mytherra with:
Lantern light in the darkness.
Mist drifting through forests.
Handwritten journal pages.
Ancient carved stones.
Fireflies on warm evenings.
Autumn leaves in mountain towns.
Wildflowers reclaiming forgotten places.
Sacred shrines glowing after restoration.
These motifs should appear throughout the game to create a recognizable visual identity.
Art Production Rules
Concept before production.
Placeholder before perfection.
Consistency over complexity.
Every asset supports the worldbuilding.
Every region has a unique visual identity.
Reuse systems, not appearances.
Beauty should invite exploration.
Definition of Success
A player should recognize a screenshot from Mytherra instantly.
The art should communicate hope, mystery, and folklore before a single line of dialogue appears.
The world itself should feel like another character—alive, memorable, and worth protecting.
